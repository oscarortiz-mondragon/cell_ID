Cell Types

Neutrophils

They are the body's primary defense against bacterial infection and physiologic stress. Normally, most of the neutrophils circulating in the bloodstream are in a mature form, with the nucleus of the cell being divided or segmented. Because of the segmented appearance of the nucleus, neutrophils are sometimes referred to as "segs." Less mature neutrophils - those that have recently been released from the bone marrow into the bloodstream - are known as "bands" or "stabs". Stab is a German term for rod.

Lymphocytes

The round, uniform nucleus and small amount of cytoplasm surrounding it are the best identifying characteristics for this cell. Lymphocytes are involved in the specific immune response including antigen-antibody reactions, and are the second most adundant cells in our body

Basophils

Basophils are intermediate in size between neutrophils and eosinophils and have simple or bilobed nuclei. They contain many coarse purple granules that can vary in size or shape.

Monocytes

Monocytes are spherical in shape or may appear amoeboid. They are large in size and have a large nucleus that is usually bean shaped. This characteristic differentiates monocytes from neutrophils, which have several lobes with a divided nucleus. In tissues, monocytes transform to macrophages.
